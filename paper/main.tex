\begin{abstract}
This paper presents an end-to-end implementation and evaluation of the
i-DECIDE two-stage deep learning framework for proactive handover (HO)
prediction in Long-Term Evolution (LTE) networks, applied to a real-world
vehicular drive-test dataset. The dataset comprises 10\,301 LTE
measurements collected over four days from a single User Equipment (UE)
traversing an urban environment, encompassing 323 handover events across
169 Physical Cell Identities (PCIs) with RSRP values spanning
$-129$ to $-60$~dBm. Stage~1 employs a stacked four-layer Long
Short-Term Memory (LSTM) network to predict future Reference Signal
Received Power (RSRP) values from 100-sample lookback windows, achieving
a test-set Mean Absolute Error (MAE) of \SI{1.44}{\dBm} and a Root Mean
Square Error (RMSE) of \SI{2.76}{\dBm}. Stage~2 encodes 50-sample windows
of LSTM-predicted RSRP into feature vectors that are classified as
handover or non-handover events by four binary classifiers---Random Forest,
Support Vector Machine (SVM) with RBF kernel, Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP),
and $k$-Nearest Neighbours (KNN)---each evaluated under a
$10\times\text{StratifiedKFold}(5)$ cross-validation protocol spanning
50 folds. Severe class imbalance (31:1) is resolved through a Tomek
Links undersampling followed by SMOTE oversampling cascade. Random Forest
achieves the highest performance with \SI{97.41}{\percent} accuracy,
\SI{97.46}{\percent} F1-score, and \SI{99.11}{\percent} recall (HO
detection rate). These results confirm that the i-DECIDE architecture
transfers successfully from controlled simulation environments to
heterogeneous real-world deployments.
\end{abstract}

\begin{IEEEkeywords}
i-DECIDE, handover prediction, LTE, LSTM, RSRP, Random Forest, SMOTE,
drive-test, proactive handover, binary classification, deep learning.
\end{IEEEkeywords}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------

\IEEEPARstart{H}{andover} (HO) management is fundamental to maintaining
seamless connectivity as a User Equipment (UE) moves across cell
boundaries in LTE networks. The 3GPP standard A3-event mechanism
compares the serving cell Reference Signal Received Power (RSRP) to that
of neighbour cells after a Time-To-Trigger (TTT) delay, making HO
decisions reactively on instantaneous measurements \cite{3gpp_36331}.
This reactive approach leads to unnecessary HOs when signal dips are
transient, ping-pong effects when a UE oscillates between two cells, and
call drops when degradation progresses faster than the TTT period.

Proactive HO prediction---predicting that a handover will be necessary
\emph{before} the A3 threshold is crossed---offers a structural
improvement: the network can pre-establish the radio link with the target
cell, allocate resources, and initiate the HO process with enough lead
time to avoid service interruption. Deep learning-based methods that
learn temporal signal patterns from historical RSRP measurements are
well-suited to this task.

This work implements and evaluates the i-DECIDE pipeline
\cite{lima2023idecide} on a real-world LTE drive-test dataset. The
i-DECIDE architecture chains an LSTM regression model (Stage~1) with a
bank of binary classifiers (Stage~2) to produce handover/non-handover
predictions from raw RSRP time series. To our knowledge, this is the
first complete evaluation of the i-DECIDE pipeline on live drive-test
measurements, as opposed to controlled simulations.

The key contributions of this paper are:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item A complete, reproducible implementation of the i-DECIDE two-stage
  deep learning pipeline applied to a real-world LTE drive-test dataset
  of 10\,301 samples collected across four days of vehicular mobility.

  \item Quantitative evaluation of the LSTM RSRP prediction stage on
  real-world channel conditions characterised by 169 distinct PCIs and
  abrupt handover-induced signal transitions.

  \item A rigorous $10\times\text{StratifiedKFold}(5)$ cross-validation
  (50 folds per classifier) of all four i-DECIDE classifiers---Random
  Forest, SVM, MLP, and KNN---on the resampled classification dataset of
  19\,604 balanced samples.

  \item Analysis of the class imbalance problem inherent to handover
  event prediction and quantitative demonstration of the Tomek
  Links + SMOTE balancing cascade prescribed in i-DECIDE.
\end{enumerate}

The remainder of the paper is organised as follows.
Section~\ref{sec:dataset} describes the drive-test dataset and
preprocessing pipeline. Section~\ref{sec:arch} presents the i-DECIDE
two-stage architecture. Section~\ref{sec:exp} details the experimental
configuration. Section~\ref{sec:results} reports and analyses all
results. Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes the paper.

\subsection{Data Collection}

A real-world LTE drive-test measurement campaign was conducted in an urban

four RAT types.

\subsection{Signal Parsing}

RSRP values in the raw CSV are stored as strings with unit suffixes
(\emph{e.g.}, ``$-99$ dBm''). Unit suffixes are stripped and values
cast to \texttt{float64}; rows with unparseable RSRP are dropped. The
dataset is sorted by ascending timestamp (the raw log was stored in
reverse chronological order) before any further processing.

\subsection{Handover Event Detection}
\label{sec:hodetect}

A handover event is labelled by detecting a change in the serving
Physical Cell Identity (PCI) between consecutive LTE samples:
\begin{equation}
  y_t =
  \begin{cases}
    1 & \text{if } \mathrm{PCI}_{t} \neq \mathrm{PCI}_{t-1} \\
    0 & \text{otherwise}
  \end{cases}
  \label{eq:ho}
\end{equation}
Applying \eqref{eq:ho} yields 323 handover events out of 10\,301 LTE
samples (HO rate: 3.14\%). Table~\ref{tab:stats} summarises the dataset
statistics.

\section{i-DECIDE Architecture}
\label{sec:arch}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------

The i-DECIDE framework \cite{lima2023idecide} is a two-stage pipeline
that converts raw RSRP time series into binary HO predictions. Stage~1
trains an LSTM to regress future RSRP values; Stage~2 classifies windows
of those predictions as HO or non-HO. Fig.~\ref{fig:arch} illustrates
the full pipeline.

\subsection{Stage 1 --- LSTM RSRP Regression}

A stacked LSTM with four layers is trained to minimise the Mean Squared
Error (MSE) between the predicted and actual RSRP:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{t=1}^{N}(\hat{x}_{t+1} - x_{t+1})^2
  \label{eq:mse}
\end{equation}
Given the input window $\mathbf{X}_t = [x_{t-99},\ldots,x_t]^\top \in
\mathbb{R}^{100}$, the network outputs the scalar prediction
$\hat{x}_{t+1}$. Table~\ref{tab:lstm} details the layer configuration.
Each LSTM layer is followed by Dropout$(0.3)$ to prevent overfitting.
The final Dense layer uses a linear activation to allow unbounded
regression output. RMSprop is used as the optimiser \cite{tieleman2012}.

\caption{i-DECIDE Stage~1 --- LSTM Layer Configuration}
\label{tab:lstm}
\centering
\begin{tabular}{llr}
\toprule
\textbf{Layer} & \textbf{Configuration} & \textbf{Parameters} \\
\midrule
Input      & shape $(100,\,1)$                           & ---     \\
LSTM-1     & 120 units, \texttt{return\_sequences=True}  & 59\,040 \\
Dropout-1  & rate $= 0.3$                                & ---     \\
LSTM-2     & 50 units, \texttt{return\_sequences=True}   & 34\,200 \\
Dropout-2  & rate $= 0.3$                                & ---     \\

\midrule

\bottomrule
\end{tabular}

\textbf{Training split.} The LTE time series is split chronologically:
the first 80\% of samples form the training set (8\,140 sequences) and
the remaining 20\% form the held-out test set (2\,061 sequences). No
random shuffling is applied in order to preserve temporal ordering.
EarlyStopping (patience~$=10$, \texttt{restore\_best\_weights=True})
monitors the validation loss and halts training when improvement stalls.

\textbf{Full-sequence inference.} After training, the LSTM performs
inference over the complete 10\,301-sample LTE series (the first 100
samples are consumed as the initial lookback) to produce 10\,201
predicted RSRP values $\hat{\mathbf{x}}$ that feed Stage~2.

\subsection{Stage 2 --- Binary Classification}

\subsubsection{Feature Construction}

A sliding window of length $W=50$ is applied to $\hat{\mathbf{x}}$:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{f}_i = [\hat{x}_i,\,\hat{x}_{i+1},\ldots,\hat{x}_{i+49}]
  ^\top \in \mathbb{R}^{50},\quad i = 1,\ldots,M
  \label{eq:win}
\end{equation}
with $M = 10\,151$ windows. Each window $\mathbf{f}_i$ is assigned the
handover label $y_i$ defined in \eqref{eq:ho}. The resulting feature
matrix has 10\,151 samples with a 50-dimensional feature vector each.

\subsubsection{Class Imbalance Correction}

The raw feature set inherits the 31:1 class ratio of the original
dataset (9\,841 non-HO vs.\ 310 HO windows). i-DECIDE prescribes
a two-step resampling cascade:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Tomek Links \cite{tomek1976}:} Pairs of samples from
  opposite classes that are mutual nearest neighbours are identified;
  the majority-class member of each pair is removed. This cleans the
  class boundary without large-scale data reduction. After this step:
  9\,802~non-HO, 310~HO.

  \item \textbf{SMOTE \cite{chawla2002}:} Synthetic minority samples
  are interpolated between existing HO windows in feature space until
  both classes are equal. After SMOTE: 9\,802~non-HO, 9\,802~HO.
\end{enumerate}
The final balanced dataset contains \textbf{19\,604 samples} at a
perfect 1:1 class ratio. All features are then standardised using
StandardScaler (zero mean, unit variance) before classifier training.

\subsubsection{Classifiers}

i-DECIDE specifies four classifiers evaluated in parallel:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Random Forest (RF):} 200 decision trees, bootstrap
  sampling, Gini impurity, parallelised over all CPU cores.

  \item \textbf{SVM (RBF):} Radial Basis Function kernel, $C=500$;
  the large regularisation enforces a tight decision boundary in the
  50-dimensional RSRP-trajectory feature space.

  \item \textbf{MLP:} Two hidden layers of 120 neurons each, ReLU
  activation, L-BFGS optimiser, $L_2$ regularisation $\alpha=10^{-4}$,
  up to 1\,000 iterations.

  \item \textbf{KNN ($k=2$):} Manhattan distance ($p=1$), ball-tree
  index for fast lookup among 50-dimensional vectors.
\end{itemize}

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:exp}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Implementation Environment}

All experiments run under Python~3.11.9 on Windows~11. The LSTM is
implemented in TensorFlow~2.20.0 / Keras~3.14.1. Scikit-learn~1.6
provides the classifiers. SMOTE and Tomek Links are sourced from
\texttt{imbalanced-learn}. Training is performed on CPU (LSTM training
time: \SI{545}{\second}; total classifier CV time: \SI{4211}{\second}).

\subsection{Cross-Validation Protocol}

All four classifiers are evaluated identically under
$10\times\text{StratifiedKFold}(5)$:

\begin{itemize}
  \item Ten independent repetitions with seeds $0$--$9$.
  \item Each repetition: stratified 5-fold cross-validation.
  \item Total folds per classifier: \textbf{50}.
  \item The 50\%/50\% class balance is preserved in every fold by
  stratification.
\end{itemize}

Metrics---accuracy, weighted F1-score, precision, recall, and the
confusion matrix---are averaged across all 50 folds. The global seed
is fixed at 42. Per-fold performance standard deviations are reported
alongside means to quantify stability.

RF achieves \SI{97.41}{\percent} accuracy and \SI{99.11}{\percent}
recall. The confusion matrix in Table~\ref{tab:cm} shows only
17.5~mean false negatives per fold---the lowest missed-HO count of
all four classifiers by a factor of at least $3\times$. This is the
primary operational metric: a missed handover prediction translates
directly to a degraded or dropped call. The corresponding false-positive
count (83.9 per fold) is a secondary concern, since spurious HO
predictions result in unnecessary but non-harmful resource reservations.

The low standard deviation of $\pm0.26\%$ on accuracy across 50 folds
confirms that RF generalises consistently and is not sensitive to the
particular random seed or fold assignment.

\subsubsection{KNN}

KNN with $k=2$ and Manhattan distance achieves \SI{96.90}{\percent}
accuracy and \SI{97.34}{\percent} recall, ranking second overall. Its
competitive performance---only $0.51\%$ below RF in accuracy---reflects
the structured nature of the 50-dimensional RSRP trajectory feature
space: pre-handover signal degradation patterns are locally consistent,
favouring proximity-based classification.

\subsubsection{MLP}

MLP attains \SI{95.09}{\percent} accuracy with \SI{95.52}{\percent}
recall. The two hidden layers of 120 neurons each provide sufficient
capacity to model the non-linear decision boundary in the 50-dimensional
space, but the L-BFGS solver's convergence is sensitive to the feature
distribution introduced by SMOTE-generated synthetic samples, contributing
to the modest gap relative to RF.

\subsubsection{SVM}

SVM (RBF, $C=500$) records \SI{88.24}{\percent} accuracy and
\SI{86.49}{\percent} recall---the lowest of the four classifiers. The
confusion matrix reveals 264.8 mean false negatives per fold,
approximately $15\times$ more than RF. The RBF kernel's implicit
assumption about the geometry of the feature space is less well-matched
to real-world RSRP trajectories, which involve abrupt transitions at PCI
changes that distort the feature manifold.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------

This paper presented a complete implementation and evaluation of the
i-DECIDE two-stage deep learning pipeline for proactive LTE handover
prediction, applied to a real-world drive-test dataset of 10\,301 LTE
measurements spanning 169 PCIs and 323 handover events.

Stage~1, a stacked four-layer LSTM (120-50-50-50 units, Dropout 0.3),
achieved an RSRP prediction MAE of \SI{1.44}{\dBm} and RMSE of
\SI{2.76}{\dBm} on the held-out test set under real-world channel
conditions. Stage~2 evaluated all four i-DECIDE binary classifiers under
a $10\times\text{StratifiedKFold}(5)$ protocol (50 folds). After Tomek
Links + SMOTE resampling resolved the 31:1 class imbalance, Random Forest
achieved \SI{97.41}{\percent} accuracy, \SI{97.46}{\percent} F1-score,
and \SI{99.11}{\percent} HO recall---the highest across all metrics and
with the lowest false-negative count (17.5 per fold). KNN, MLP, and SVM
followed in that order.

These results demonstrate that the i-DECIDE pipeline is robust to the
increased channel complexity of real-world deployments---including
irregular signal transitions, urban multipath fading, and high
cell-density interference---making it a viable component for proactive
handover management in operational LTE and emerging 5G networks.

Future directions include: (i)~multi-step RSRP prediction to extend the
handover prediction horizon beyond one sample; (ii)~incorporating
additional radio measurements (RSRQ, SINR, CQI) as auxiliary input
features; (iii)~online (incremental) retraining of the LSTM as new drive
measurements arrive; and (iv)~porting the pipeline to a 5G NR measurement
framework using 3GPP NR SSB-based signal reports.
