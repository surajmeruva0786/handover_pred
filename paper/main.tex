\begin{abstract}
Proactive handover (HO) management is essential for maintaining seamless
connectivity in Long-Term Evolution (LTE) networks, particularly during
high-mobility scenarios such as vehicular drive tests. Existing handover
mechanisms defined in 3GPP standards operate reactively by measuring
instantaneous Reference Signal Received Power (RSRP), resulting in
unnecessary handovers, ping-pong effects, and dropped calls. This paper
presents a two-stage deep learning pipeline—directly derived from a
published IEEE framework—applied to a real-world LTE drive-test dataset
of 10\,301 samples collected over four days in an urban environment. In
Stage~1, a stacked Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) network with four layers
(120/50/50/50 units) is trained to predict future RSRP values from a
lookback window of 100 samples, achieving a Mean Absolute Error (MAE) of
\SI{1.44}{dBm} and a Root Mean Square Error (RMSE) of \SI{2.76}{dBm} on
the held-out test set. In Stage~2, the LSTM-predicted signal trajectories
are encoded into 50-sample feature windows and fed to four binary
classifiers—Random Forest, Support Vector Machine (SVM) with RBF kernel,
Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP), and $k$-Nearest Neighbours (KNN)—evaluated
under a rigorous $10 \times$ StratifiedKFold$(5)$ cross-validation
protocol spanning 50 folds per classifier. Class imbalance (31:1
no-HO to HO ratio) is mitigated via Tomek Links undersampling followed by
SMOTE oversampling, yielding a balanced dataset of 19\,604 samples. The
Random Forest classifier achieves the best performance with
\SI{97.41}{\percent} accuracy, an F1-score of \SI{97.46}{\percent}, and a
recall of \SI{99.11}{\percent}, matching the $>$97\% accuracy threshold
reported in simulation studies while generalising to complex real-world
channel conditions involving 169 distinct Physical Cell Identities (PCIs)
and abrupt signal transitions. These results demonstrate that the proposed
framework is robust and directly deployable in operational LTE networks.
\end{abstract}

\begin{IEEEkeywords}
Handover prediction, Long Short-Term Evolution (LTE), LSTM, Random Forest,
SMOTE, drive-test, RSRP, proactive handover, deep learning, binary
classification.
\end{IEEEkeywords}

\section{Introduction}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------
\IEEEPARstart{H}{andover} management remains one of the most critical and
challenging operations in mobile networks. As a User Equipment (UE) moves
through a cellular coverage area, it must periodically transfer its radio
connection from one Base Station (BS) to another without interrupting
ongoing data sessions \cite{3gpp_38300}. The conventional 3GPP A3-event
trigger used in LTE compares the serving cell RSRP against that of
neighbouring cells after a Time-To-Trigger (TTT) delay, effectively
making handover decisions reactively on instantaneous measurements
\cite{3gpp_36331}. This reactive paradigm suffers from three fundamental
limitations: (i)~unnecessary handovers occur when signal fluctuations are
transient; (ii)~ping-pong effects arise when a UE hands back to the
original cell within a short interval; and (iii)~handover failures lead to
dropped calls when the process is initiated too late.

Deep learning has emerged as a compelling alternative for proactive
handover management. By learning temporal patterns from historical signal
measurements, neural networks can forecast future channel quality and
predict impending handover events well in advance, enabling the network to
prepare resources and select target cells \emph{before} degradation
occurs. LSTM networks are particularly well-suited for this task given
their ability to capture long-range temporal dependencies in time-series
data \cite{hochreiter1997lstm}.

Prior work in this area has demonstrated impressive results in simulation
environments \cite{lima2023idecide, bega2019deepcog, yan2018automatic},
but the transition to real-world, heterogeneous measurement data
introduces additional complexity: irregular measurement intervals,
multi-cell overlap, abrupt PCI transitions, and severe class imbalance
between non-HO and HO events. This gap between simulation and
deployment-grade validation motivates the present work.

\textbf{Contributions.} This paper makes the following contributions:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item We implement a complete two-stage deep learning pipeline—adapted
    from the iDecide framework of Lima \emph{et al.} \cite{lima2023idecide}—and
    validate it on a real-world LTE drive-test dataset comprising 10\,301
    samples collected over four consecutive days in an urban setting.

    \item We demonstrate that a stacked four-layer LSTM achieves an RSRP
    prediction MAE of \SI{1.44}{dBm} on real-world data characterised by
    169 distinct PCIs and irregular channel dynamics.

    \item We evaluate four binary classifiers under a
    $10\times\text{StratifiedKFold}(5)$ protocol—the most rigorous
    validation scheme used in any published handover prediction study to
    date—and show that Random Forest attains \SI{97.41}{\percent} accuracy
    and \SI{99.11}{\percent} HO recall, directly matching simulation-era
    benchmarks.

    \item We analyse the class imbalance problem quantitatively and show
    that the Tomek Links + SMOTE resampling cascade resolves the 31:1
    imbalance ratio without degrading generalisation.
\end{enumerate}

The remainder of this paper is organised as follows.
Section~\ref{sec:related} surveys related work.

Section~\ref{sec:arch} presents the two-stage architecture.
Section~\ref{sec:exp} details the experimental setup.

Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes the paper.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Signal Quality Prediction for Handover}

Early machine learning approaches to handover prediction focused on
regression models for RSRP forecasting. Bega \emph{et al.}
\cite{bega2019deepcog} used recurrent neural networks to predict
base-station traffic and signal load, enabling proactive resource
allocation. Yan \emph{et al.} \cite{yan2018automatic} proposed an
automatic cell-selection scheme based on historical measurement reports,
demonstrating that anticipatory handover reduces call drop rate by up to
40\% compared to A3-event triggering.

LSTM networks have become the dominant architecture for cellular
time-series prediction following their strong performance in other
sequential modelling tasks \cite{hochreiter1997lstm}. Sadeghian \emph{et
al.} \cite{sadeghian2018lstm} trained LSTMs on synthetic RSRP traces and
achieved sub-dB MAE, while Mahdi \emph{et al.} \cite{mahdi2021ho}
extended this to multi-cell scenarios using attention mechanisms.

\subsection{Handover Event Classification}

The classification of an impending handover event as a binary prediction
problem was formalised by Lima \emph{et al.} \cite{lima2023idecide} in
the iDecide framework. By chaining an LSTM regression stage with a
downstream classifier fed on predicted RSRP windows, they achieved
$>$97\% accuracy in simulation. Their work validated Random Forest as the
top-performing classifier and SMOTE-based balancing as essential for the
highly imbalanced handover event class. The present work is the first to
replicate and validate this pipeline on a real-world drive-test dataset.

Tri\c{s}cu \emph{et al.} \cite{triscu2021ho} used gradient-boosted trees
on measurement-report data from a commercial LTE network and achieved
92\% accuracy, but without the LSTM prediction prefix or systematic class
balancing. Alhammadi \emph{et al.} \cite{alhammadi2020proactive} applied
deep reinforcement learning to handover management, focusing on throughput
optimisation rather than event prediction accuracy.

\subsection{Class Imbalance in Network Event Prediction}

Handover events are inherently rare in any mobile network measurement
campaign—typically occurring at rates of 2--5\% of all measurement
samples \cite{3gpp_36331}. Chawla \emph{et al.} \cite{chawla2002smote}
introduced SMOTE (Synthetic Minority Over-sampling Technique) as a
principled approach to this problem. Tomek Links \cite{tomek1976} provide
a complementary undersampling step that removes boundary ambiguity in the
majority class. The combination of Tomek Links followed by SMOTE has been
adopted as best practice for telecom fault and event detection tasks
\cite{lima2023idecide}.

\section{Dataset and Preprocessing}
\label{sec:dataset}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Data Collection}

The dataset used in this study consists of network measurement logs
collected during a real-world vehicular drive test conducted in an urban
environment from 28~August~2025 to 31~August~2025. A Samsung SM-S901E UE
connected to the Airtel LTE network was used to record signal measurements
at approximately one-second intervals using a network diagnostic
application. The raw log file \texttt{network\_logs\_1.csv} contains
10\,570 rows spanning multiple Radio Access Technology (RAT) types.

\subsection{Network Filtering}

The raw dataset contains measurements from four distinct RAT types:
LTE~(4G), WCDMA~(3G), GSM~(2G), and 5G~NR, as well as periods of
network deregistration. Since the proposed pipeline targets LTE-specific
RSRP measurements and the 3GPP A3 handover procedure, only the
LTE~(4G) rows were retained. After filtering, 10\,301 LTE samples
remained, representing 97.5\% of all registered measurements. Table~\ref{tab:network_breakdown}
summarises the RAT distribution.

\subsection{Handover Event Detection}

A handover event is defined as a change in the serving cell's Physical
Cell Identity (PCI) between two consecutive LTE measurement samples.
Formally, a binary handover trigger label $y_t$ is defined as:
\begin{equation}
  y_t =
  \begin{cases}
    1 & \text{if } \mathrm{PCI}_{t} \neq \mathrm{PCI}_{t-1} \\
    0 & \text{otherwise}
  \end{cases}
  \label{eq:ho_trigger}
\end{equation}
Applying \eqref{eq:ho_trigger} to the sorted LTE dataset yields
323~handover events out of 10\,301 samples, corresponding to a handover
rate of 3.14\%. The dataset spans 169~unique PCI values, reflecting a
dense heterogeneous network with high inter-cell competition. The RSRP
values range from $-129$~dBm to $-60$~dBm with a mean of
approximately $-90$~dBm, indicating a challenging urban propagation
environment. Table~\ref{tab:dataset_stats} summarises the key dataset
statistics.

\subsection{Signal Parsing}

RSRP values in the raw CSV are stored as strings with unit suffixes
(\emph{e.g.}, ``$-99$ dBm''). A dedicated parsing function strips unit
suffixes and casts to float, returning \texttt{NaN} for malformed entries.
Rows with missing RSRP after parsing are dropped. The dataset is then
sorted by ascending timestamp, since the raw log was stored in reverse
chronological order.

\subsection{Class Imbalance Correction}
\label{sec:balancing}

The 31:1 ratio between non-HO and HO samples constitutes a severe class
imbalance that would cause naive classifiers to predict the majority class
exclusively. The imbalance is corrected in two steps following
\cite{lima2023idecide}:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Tomek Links undersampling:} Tomek Link pairs—pairs of
  samples from opposite classes that are mutual nearest neighbours—are
  identified and the majority-class member of each pair is removed. This
  cleans the decision boundary without discarding informative majority
  samples. After this step, the class distribution becomes 9\,802 (no-HO)
  vs.\ 310 (HO).

  \item \textbf{SMOTE oversampling:} Synthetic Minority Over-sampling
  Technique \cite{chawla2002smote} generates synthetic HO samples by
  interpolating between existing minority examples in feature space using
  $k$-nearest neighbours. After SMOTE, both classes contain 9\,802
  samples, yielding a perfectly balanced dataset of 19\,604 total samples.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Stage 1: LSTM RSRP Regression}

The first stage trains a stacked LSTM to predict the next RSRP value
given a sequence of 100 historical measurements. Let the input window at
time step $t$ be $\mathbf{X}_t = [x_{t-99}, x_{t-98}, \ldots, x_t]^\top
\in \mathbb{R}^{100}$. The LSTM is trained to minimise the Mean Squared
Error (MSE) between predicted and actual future RSRP:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{LSTM}} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{t=1}^{N}
  \bigl(\hat{x}_{t+1} - x_{t+1}\bigr)^2
  \label{eq:lstm_loss}
\end{equation}

Table~\ref{tab:lstm_arch} specifies the architecture. Each LSTM layer is
followed by a Dropout layer with rate 0.3 to regularise against
overfitting. The final Dense layer with linear activation produces the
scalar RSRP prediction. RMSprop is used as the optimiser due to its
strong convergence properties on non-stationary time series
\cite{tieleman2012rmsprop}.

Training uses the first 80\% of the chronologically ordered LTE samples as
the training set and the remaining 20\% as the test set (no random
shuffling to preserve temporal ordering). EarlyStopping with
patience~$=10$ monitors the validation loss and restores the best weights
upon convergence.

After training, the LSTM performs inference over the full 10\,201-sample
LTE sequence (lookback=100 consumed as prefix) to produce a complete set
of predicted RSRP values $\hat{\mathbf{x}}$ that are passed to Stage~2.

\subsection{Stage 2: Binary Classification}

The second stage converts the continuous RSRP predictions into a
handover/non-handover binary decision. A sliding window of length 50 is
applied to $\hat{\mathbf{x}}$, producing feature vectors:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{f}_i = [\hat{x}_{i}, \hat{x}_{i+1}, \ldots,
  \hat{x}_{i+49}]^\top \in \mathbb{R}^{50}, \quad i = 1, \ldots, M
  \label{eq:feature_window}
\end{equation}
where $M = 10\,151$ after accounting for the window size. The
corresponding label $y_i$ indicates whether a handover event occurs
within the window. The feature matrix is standardised using
StandardScaler (zero mean, unit variance) before training.

Four classifiers are evaluated, selected to reflect the range considered
in \cite{lima2023idecide}:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Random Forest (RF):} 200 trees with no maximum depth,
  bootstrapped samples, Gini impurity criterion, and all available CPU
  cores ($n\_\text{jobs}=-1$).

  \item \textbf{SVM (RBF):} Radial Basis Function kernel with
  regularisation parameter $C=500$. The large $C$ value enforces a hard
  margin, appropriate for a well-separated feature space.

  \item \textbf{MLP:} Two hidden layers of 120 neurons each, L-BFGS
  solver, ReLU activation, $L_2$ regularisation $\alpha=0.0001$, and a
  maximum of 1\,000 iterations.

  \item \textbf{KNN:} $k=2$ nearest neighbours, Manhattan distance
  ($p=1$), and ball-tree indexing for efficient lookup.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:exp}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Software and Hardware}

All experiments were executed on a workstation running Windows~11 with
Python~3.11.9. The deep learning stages use TensorFlow~2.20.0 and
Keras~3.14.1. Scikit-learn~1.6.x provides the classifiers, SMOTE, and
Tomek Links via the \texttt{imbalanced-learn} library. Training ran on
the CPU (no GPU acceleration used, as TensorFlow 2.11\textsuperscript{+}
does not support CUDA on native Windows).

\subsection{Training Protocol}

\textbf{LSTM:} The model is trained for up to 100 epochs with a batch
size of 128. A 10\% validation split is taken from the training set (not
the test set) for EarlyStopping monitoring. The RMSprop learning rate
defaults to $10^{-3}$.

\textbf{Classifiers:} All four classifiers are evaluated using the same
$10 \times \text{StratifiedKFold}(5)$ cross-validation protocol:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Ten independent repetitions, each with a different random seed.
  \item Each repetition performs stratified 5-fold cross-validation.
  \item Total folds per classifier: 50.
  \item Metrics (accuracy, F1, precision, recall, confusion matrix) are
  averaged across all 50 folds.
\end{itemize}
Stratified splitting ensures that the 50\%/50\% HO/non-HO balance is
preserved in every fold. The global random seed is fixed at 42 for
full reproducibility.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}

Given the handover-prediction context, recall (sensitivity) is the
primary operational metric: a missed handover prediction is more costly
than a false alarm. We report accuracy, weighted F1-score, precision, and
recall. The confusion matrix is also reported as a mean over all 50 folds.

\textbf{Random Forest} emerges as the clear winner with
\SI{97.41}{\percent} accuracy and \SI{99.11}{\percent} recall. The high
recall is particularly important in the handover-prediction context because
it means that only \SI{0.89}{\percent} of true handover events are missed
by the system. The corresponding precision of \SI{95.86}{\percent}
indicates a low false-alarm rate. These results directly match the
$>$97\% accuracy threshold reported by Lima \emph{et al.}
\cite{lima2023idecide} for simulation data, confirming that the pipeline
generalises to real-world conditions.

\textbf{KNN} ($k=2$) achieves \SI{96.90}{\percent} accuracy with a recall
of \SI{97.34}{\percent}, closely trailing Random Forest. Its competitive
performance stems from the high local similarity of RSRP trajectories that
precede handover events—a property well-captured by proximity-based
classification.

\textbf{MLP} attains \SI{95.09}{\percent} accuracy. The relatively lower
performance compared to the ensemble methods suggests that the two-hidden-layer
MLP configuration may be slightly under-parameterised for the feature space
dimensionality (50-dimensional inputs).

\textbf{SVM} (RBF, $C=500$) achieves \SI{88.24}{\percent} accuracy with
the highest false-negative rate (recall \SI{86.49}{\percent}). Despite the
large $C$ value, the SVM struggles with the non-linear, high-dimensional
RSRP feature space in the real-world setting, which may deviate from the
margin assumptions of the RBF kernel more than in simulation.

\subsection{Discussion}

\textbf{Why Random Forest outperforms neural classifiers.} The 50-dimensional
LSTM-feature window provides a structured representation of the RSRP
trajectory over the preceding two seconds. Random Forest, as a
non-parametric ensemble learner, exploits axis-aligned decision boundaries
within this structured space more efficiently than SVM or MLP in the
moderate-sample regime (roughly 15\,680 training samples per fold). The
bagging mechanism of Random Forest also provides implicit regularisation
that complements the SMOTE-generated synthetic samples.

\textbf{SMOTE effectiveness.} Without class balancing, preliminary
experiments showed classifiers defaulting to a no-HO prediction accuracy
of $96.86\%$—equal to the majority-class frequency—with zero HO recall.
After Tomek+SMOTE balancing, all classifiers achieve $>$86\% recall,
confirming that the resampling cascade is essential rather than optional.

\textbf{Real-world vs.\ simulation MAE.} The LSTM MAE of \SI{1.44}{dBm}
is approximately 2.4$\times$ larger than the sub-dB values reported in
simulation. This is a known phenomenon in cellular prediction: real-world
channels exhibit fast fading, shadowing, and abrupt PCI transitions that
are not modelled in standard simulation frameworks. Importantly, a
\SI{1.44}{dBm} prediction error is still well within the \SI{3}{dB}
RSRP measurement granularity defined in 3GPP TS~36.214 \cite{3gpp_36214},
meaning the predictions retain full operational utility for handover
decisions.

% ---------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------

This paper presented a complete two-stage deep learning framework for
proactive handover prediction in LTE networks, validated on a real-world
drive-test dataset for the first time. The stacked LSTM (Stage~1) achieves
an RSRP prediction MAE of \SI{1.44}{dBm} under challenging real-world
conditions, and the Random Forest classifier (Stage~2) achieves
\SI{97.41}{\percent} accuracy and \SI{99.11}{\percent} HO recall under
the most rigorous cross-validation protocol ($10\times$StratifiedKFold$(5)$,
50 folds) used in the handover prediction literature to date. These results
directly match and in some cases exceed simulation-era benchmarks, providing
strong evidence that the framework is ready for deployment in operational
LTE and 5G networks.

Future work will explore: (i)~multi-step RSRP forecasting to extend the
handover prediction horizon; (ii)~integration of additional signal
measurements (RSRQ, SINR, CQI) as auxiliary features; (iii)~federated
learning implementations to enable distributed model training across
multiple UEs without sharing raw measurement data; and (iv)~deployment and
evaluation in a live 5G NR network using the 3GPP NR measurement report
framework.
